Online orders in Hong Kong for the much-hyped Apple iPhone X ended after just 12 minutes yesterday, while mainland buyers may have to wait up to six weeks for their handsets.


At 3.13pm - 12 minutes after Apple's Hong Kong site began taking orders at 3.01pm - all variants of the gadget were snapped up. Many early purchasers were believed to be touts hoping to make a generous profit reselling the phones, which start at HK$8,588.


A separate website allowing people to reserve and pick up their handsets at an Apple Store was also closed in under 10 minutes.


"We're not taking reservations to buy iPhone right now. You can shop online now and select another purchase option," the website read. "Reservations will reopen at 8am on November 4."


A small group of buyers managed to get in and reserve their phones for collection on November 3. But most mainland buyers were told delivery would not happen until December, even though Apple will begin shipping the device on November 3.


"To book an iPhone X is easy but I have to wait five weeks [until early December] to get the phone," said Eric Li, a Shenzhen-based analyst who ordered an iPhone X online on the official Apple website for 9,688 yuan (HK$11,380).


Li said he was able to complete the transaction on the mainland site, an indication that demand was lower, whereas attempts via the Hong Kong website failed.


"Actually the muted situation is not what I expected. Maybe the iPhone X is too expensive for most people. It is about 1,300 yuan cheaper in Hong Kong than the mainland. Maybe most buyers want to buy it in Hong Kong," Li said.


The strong demand, coupled with limited supply, means there is likely to be a robust secondary market. There is speculation that those who can get their hands on the phone in the first days of the launch could make more than HK$10,000 in profit.


However, professional traders at Sincere Podium in Mong Kok were cautious about fixing a price too early. Alex Yeung of Mobile Empire said the price was set by cunning distributors and authorised resellers who were reluctant to sell the phones in-store.
